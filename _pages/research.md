## How cities shape the brain

I'm a PhD student at UCL's [Spiers Lab](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/bartlett/news/2024/sep/spiers-lab) within the Bartlett Faculty of the Built Environment. My research sits at the intersection of cognitive neuroscience, urban studies, and environmental psychology — a field increasingly known as **neurourbanism**.

The core question I work on: *how does the physical design of our environment — streets, buildings, parks, neighbourhoods — affect cognitive function, spatial memory, and psychological well-being?*

---

## Current project

My PhD investigates the cognitive and neural consequences of different urban environments. I focus on how architectural and spatial features (scale, complexity, greenery, enclosure) modulate human memory, stress, and navigation.

Working methods include behavioural experiments, EEG, eye-tracking, and virtual environment paradigms — tools that let us study human cognition in ecologically valid settings that approximate real-world urban experience.

---

## Background

My path into neurourbanism was indirect. I started with human geography (Université de Poitiers, exchange at UQAM Montréal), moved into cognitive neuroscience (Universiteit van Amsterdam, MSc Brain and Cognitive Sciences), and along the way completed research internships at the [Max Planck Institute for Human Development](https://www.mpib-berlin.mpg.de/en) in Berlin and the Conscious Brain Lab in Amsterdam.

My master's thesis — graded 9/10 — examined nature, aesthetic preference, and psychological restoration. It planted the seed for thinking seriously about how designed environments shape inner experience.

## Broader interests

- **Spatial cognition**: how humans build, store, and navigate mental maps
- **Restorative environments**: what makes some spaces feel calming and others stressful
- **Urban planning implications**: translating neuroscience findings into evidence-based design
- **Primatology**: field experience studying macaque behaviour in Malaysian rainforest (Universiti Sains Malaysia, 2019)
